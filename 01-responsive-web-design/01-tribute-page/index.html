Peter Higgs

A well-recognized lifetime of contribution to physics

A timeline of Peter Higgs' most notable achievements:

  • 1929: born in Elswick, Newcastle upon Tyne, United Kingdom.
  • 1941-46: attends Cotham Grammar School in Bristol.
  • 1950: graduates with a first class honours degree in Physics from King's College London.
  • 1954: gets awarded a PhD for his thesis: "Some Problems in the Theory of Molecular Vibrations."
  • 1954-56: gets appointed as a Senior Research Fellow at the University of Edinburgh.
  • 1960: returns to the University of Edinburgh, taking up the post of Lecturer at the Tait Institute of Mathematical Physics.
  • 1964: publishes the paper titled "Broken Symmetries, Massless Particles and Gauge Fields," in Physics Letters 12, and then "Broken Symmetries and the Masses of Gauge Bosons," in Physical Review Letters 13, where he predicts a new massive spin-zero boson.
  • 1974: becomes a Fellow of the Fellowship of the Royal Society of Edinburgh (FRSE).
  • 1983: gets elected as a Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS).
  • 1984: gets awarded the Rutherford Medal and Prize.
  • 1996: retires and becomes Emeritus Professor at the University of Edinburgh.
  • 1997: receives an honorary degree from the University of Bristol.
  • 2008: receives an Honorary Fellowship from Swansea University.
  • 2012: CERN announces that the existence of a Higgs-like boson has been experimentally established.
  • 2013: the newly discovered particle was tentatively confirmed to be + parity and zero spin, two fundamental criteria of a Higgs boson, making it the first known fundamental scalar particle to be discovered in nature.
  • 2013: shares the Nobel Prize in Physics with Francois Englebert "for the theoretical discovery of a mechanism that contributes to our understanding of the origin of mass of subatomic particles".
